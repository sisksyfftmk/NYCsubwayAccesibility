Are New Yorkers Strong? The Reality of Subway Accessibility

Fact-Checking a Comedian's Joke

By Yohei

While attending a comedy show in New York, I heard a comedian joke that the city's subways lack elevators because "elevators are for the weak, and New Yorkers are strong." While intended as humor, this sparked an idea to take a data journalism approach to fact-check this premise. Does the lack of accessibility truly reflect the city's infrastructure, and who bears the burden of this "strength"? 

Across New York City, subway accessibility remains limited. According to the MTA dataset, out of 473 total subway stations in the transit system, only 114 are classified as accessible, resulting in an overall system accessibility rate of 24.1%.

When broken down by borough, Manhattan contains the highest number of accessible stations at 39 out of 121 (32.2%). Queens follows with 19 accessible stations out of 78 (24.4%), and the Bronx has 20 out of 68 (29.4%). Brooklyn has 34 accessible stations out of 156 total stations, yielding an accessibility rate of 21.8%.

These figures confirm that less than one-third of the subway stations across all five boroughs provide elevator access.

Despite these low accessibility rates, U.S. Census demographic data shows a consistent need for accessible infrastructure across all five boroughs. Children under 5 and seniors aged 65 and over account for approximately 20% of the total population in every borough.

In Brooklyn, this combined group represents 21.3% of the population, followed by Manhattan at 21.8%, Queens at 21.6%, the Bronx at 20.2%, and Staten Island at 22.9%.

Among the five boroughs, Brooklyn demands particular attention. Its accessibility rate is strikingly low, while the proportion of children under five is the second highest. This points to a clear mismatch between supply and demand.
